    Study of the domain

    The credit for everything that characterizes a book nowadays, except for ink and paper, belongs to the first editor in history: Aldo Manuzio. He introduced the need to read, also and mainly for enjoyment, during the free time and claimed the reader's right to freely interpret the text, because he stopped the tradition according to which the original text of a work had to be accompanied by different comments offering precise interpretations. No editor other than Manuzio was able to create a social network as wide as his, indeed he used prefaces and inscriptions as promotional tools for the books. He introduced the catalog, the summary with numeration of the pages, the italic font and widely contributed to the spread of the pocket-sized books. Aldo Manuzio was born in Bassiano, nowadays situated in the province of Latina, around 1450 and died in 1515 in Venice. In 1489 he moved to Venice, the perfect cradle for his “book revolution”: as a Republic, Venice didn’t have the strong oppression of the censorship or of a noble government, the city was rich of sweet water which was important for the production of paper, and the presence of two public schools, that of San Marco and that of Rialto, and the university of Padova (the nearest for those who intended to get a degree) influenced the high demand of books. Thus, Manuzio started in Venice his printing office, the Aldine Press. We can say Manuzio was the first editor ever because before him, typographers selected works to publish according to their potential as a product to sell, without an editorial project and a cultural program with a minimum care for the quality of content.

    Geographical representation of Venice created and published by Jacopo de Barbari in 1500. The city was historically the capital of the Republic of Venice for over a millennium. It was a major financial and maritime power during the Middle Ages and Renaissance. Venice was rich in printers and editors, a feature that attracted intellectuals and writers, who took advantage of the presence of immigrants to print books in different languages

    I libri proibiti

    Book

    I libri proibiti: da Gutemberg all'Encyclopédie, book by Mario Infelise. While in Italy the Renaissance was developing, in Germany the protestant reformation was taking action, becoming the first big conflict in which the role of the printing press has been fundamental. In 1517 Lutero published his 95 theses, which led to the spread of translations and prints of the Bible. The response of the Romanic Church was the creation of the Index of prohibited books and the introduction, in 1542, of the Inquisition, the only courthouse operating during the modern age in Italy

    Document of censorship

    Document

    Document of 1664 by Pope Alexander VII about book censorship and Index of prohibited books. The censorship greatly damaged the development of studies, discoveries, and the economic condition of book sellers, because it expanded widely beyond religion, affecting all fields of knowledge. The first intervention of preventive censorship on the printing press took place in 1472, when the Italian bishop Perotti suggested a committee who had the power to authorize the publication of classics’ editions. Time after time, governments started to contribute to the censorship and controls over writing, printing and publishing became oppressive. In 1487 the Inter multiplices law of Pope Innocenzo VIII prohibited the spread of books “contrary to religion and morals” and in 1501 the Inter sollicitudines law of Pope Alessandro VI Borgia introduced the imprimatur, the religious authorization to the printing, so that just accepted books could be published. The Index of prohibited books was, instead, a list of specific books which couldn’t be printed, held, or read by anyone. The first edition of the Index was published in 1559 and after that several editions followed, until 1966, the year in which Pope Paolo VI abrogated it

    Hypnerotomachia Poliphili

    Book

    In 1499, Manuzio printed Hypnerotomachia Poliphili, which is considered the most beautiful book of the Renaissance and narrated the love of Polifilo, the protagonist, towards Polia, a woman who, hit by the plague, made vows of chastity and rejected Polifilo. The layout of the text was creative, indeed, the words created different shapes: columns, pyramids, goblets and so on.The engravings were 172 and their author unknown. Some drawing had an erotic content and this book presented the first illustration of a man’s genitals. The author of the Polifilo is unknown, but joining the initial letters of the thirty eight chapters, the name of Francesco Colonna was obtained

    Aldus Pagemaker

    Software

    In 1985, in Seattle, the Aldus Corporation of Paul Brainerd, inspired by Aldo Manuzio*, created the first program for an electronic layout: Aldus PageMaker, of which the first version was used by Apple computers. In 1994, the society was bought by Adobe and the program became Adobe PageMaker, predecessor of the present InDesign

    Logotype of Aldine Press

    Logotype

    Reproduction of engravings of the Aldine Press’ logotype, created by Tomaso Filippi, held by the Civic Museum of Venice. It is possible that the engravings of Polifilo inspired the typographical brand of Aldo Manuzio: with this work were printed for the first time the motto “festina tarde” (“slowly hurry up”, which became then “festina lente”) and the anchor with the dolphin. The drawing reflected in a graphic shape the meaning of the Latin motto, because the dolphin symbolized the speed while the anchor represented the slowness. Manuzio chose this symbol because his editorial engagement meant to be fast but accurate at the same time
